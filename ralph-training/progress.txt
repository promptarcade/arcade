## Lessons from pre-ralph training (2026-03-27/28):

### Sphere lessons:
- Phong/Blinn-Phong specular lands in wrong spot — use pow(diffuse, N) to keep it co-located with bright side
- Ground planes need horizon (full canvas fill), not a rectangle with hard edges
- Sink spheres 1-2px into ground to hide circle aliasing at poles
- Cast shadows need radius ~1.2x object, intensity ~0.75 — defaults are always too subtle
- Low ambient (0.03) creates spotlight look — use 0.12-0.18 for dispersed light
- CRITICAL: smooth surfaces expose palette banding. Perturb normals with bump noise (0.1-0.2 strength) to break concentric bands. This is WHY the dragon eye references look good — every scale catches light differently.

### Colour lessons:
- Keep hue range TIGHT — a red sphere should read as RED throughout, not white-to-purple
- Single palette with engine hue-shifting is better than dual-palette switching (avoids seam)
- Specular accent palette only for the tightest highlight, not a broad zone

### Construction lessons:
- Handle attachments must not create steps — extend handles past belly peak
- Profile functions need smooth transitions — no piecewise linear with hard breakpoints
- Use proven canvas sizes (128x96 for HD props, ~46px half-widths)
- Subtle belly (2-3px) not dramatic (6px+)

### Proven code patterns (from reference_art_code_patterns.md — USE THESE):
- drawPost setup: outlineMode:'none', dummy pixel in draw(), clear in drawPost, tone() helper
- Sphere lighting: nx=dx/r, ny=dy/r, nz=sqrt(1-nx²-ny²), NdotL, diffuse, Phong specular, S-curve
- Cylinder lighting: nx=dx/halfW, nz=sqrt(1-nx²), same NdotL formula but no ny
- Dual palette: warm group (v>0.38) and cool group for shadows
- Profile functions: define silhouette as halfWidth(t), render flat, verify, THEN shade
- Tight packing: rowSpacing = formSize - 2, jitter 30%, sort by Y, dark crevice base
- Bump texture: perturb normals with (hash-0.5)*strength before computing NdotL
- Fresnel glass: thickness = abs(dx/halfW), fresnel = pow(1-nz, 2)*0.5+0.12
- Metal: high specular (80-120 exp), environment reflection, quintic S-curve

### Key rendering principles (from reference_art_fundamentals.md):
- NO EMPTY SPACE — every pixel belongs to a rendered surface
- FULL TONAL RANGE PER FORM — each form needs near-white highlight AND near-black shadow
- EDGES DEFINE FORMS — concentrate contrast at boundaries, not gradient fills
- COLOUR TEMPERATURE SHIFTS — lit=warm, shadow=cool, separate palette groups
- CONSISTENT DETAIL DENSITY — texture must be uniform everywhere
- SPECULAR: tiny and extreme contrast (2-5px, pure white against dark)

### Existing scale rendering (from tools/props/training/p5-6-scales.js):
- Each scale is a distinct 3D convex form with own highlight/shadow
- Dark crevices between scales (near-black)
- Size varies: large plates on flat areas, tiny near joints
- Overlapping: bottom edge of upper scale covers top of lower
- Row overlap spacing is critical for reading as a surface

### Meta lessons:
- ALWAYS check qa-knowledge.json before starting
- ALWAYS compare output against dragon eye references before marking done
- Drawing skill doesn't transfer between sessions — only principles do
- The gap between knowing rules and applying them resets every session
- Study existing approved outputs BEFORE drawing, not after

### NEW TRANSFERABLE DISCOVERIES:

1. **BUMP ON NORMALS COMPRESSES TONAL RANGE**
   - Perturbing normals before computing NdotL redistributes lighting — dark pixels get brighter, bright pixels get darker
   - Net effect: mid-tone compression, reducing the visible tonal range
   - FIX: Compute CLEAN lighting first (smooth dome), then modulate FINAL brightness with noise
   - This preserves the full tonal sweep while adding surface variation
   - Applies to: ANY form where full tonal range matters (most things)

2. **2-GROUP PALETTE FOR CONTINUOUS RAMP**
   - With 5 palette groups × 8 tones, each group covers a narrow brightness band
   - Transitions between groups create jumps; most tones go unused
   - 2 groups × 8 tones = 16-tone continuous ramp from cool-dark to warm-bright
   - Cool group covers v=0..0.42, warm group covers v=0.42..1.0
   - Applies to: any single-form study where continuous gradation matters

3. **FEATURE-BASED TEXTURE, NOT NOISE-BASED**
   - Hash noise at ANY scale (1px, 3px, 4px blocks) reads as dithering or grid artifacts
   - The references achieve texture through visible MICRO-STRUCTURES: tiny bumps, pits, scratches
   - Each micro-feature needs its own highlight and shadow (2-3px highlight pixel + shadow pixel)
   - This is fundamentally different from noise perturbation — it's drawing, not math
   - Applies to: any organic surface (skin, bark, stone, leather)

4. **SPECULAR SHAPE MATTERS**
   - Cross/plus/star shapes read as UI cursor or lens flare
   - Irregular L-shaped or asymmetric clusters read as wet/glossy glint
   - 2-3px maximum, with the absolute brightest palette value
   - Must sit on surface at 20-30% brightness, NOT in the highlight zone
   - Applies to: any specular on any surface

5. **HARDCODED PALETTE EXTREMES > COMPUTED VALUES**
   - Computing v → mapping through palette → tone(group, v) often produces soft extremes
   - For edge pairs and specular, use raw palette indices: DARK = group.startIdx, BRIGHT = group.startIdx + toneCount - 1
   - These are LITERAL palette extremes that bypass all computation
   - Applies to: any case where you need guaranteed extreme contrast

6. **GROOVE RIDGES: CONTEXT vs EVERYWHERE**
   - Critic said ridges should be "near-max EVERYWHERE" — but near-white ridges in deep shadow zones look like floating lines
   - Better approach: ridges should be the LOCALLY brightest element, not globally brightest
   - Shadow-zone ridges at 40-50% brightness still read as ridges if the crevice is near-black
   - The PAIR is what matters, not the absolute brightness of either element
   - Applies to: any dark-bright edge pair in varying lighting zones

### KEY BREAKTHROUGH: Understanding the ColorRamp algorithm

The engine's `ColorRamp.generateN()` creates 8 tones from a base hex by:
1. Converting to HSL
2. Creating 4 anchors: deepShadow (L*0.45, H+20°), shadow (L*0.70, H+10°), base (L), highlight (L*1.30, H-15°)
3. Interpolating tones across these anchors with smoothstep

**This means the tonal range is a FUNCTION of the base hex's lightness:**
- Near-black base (#020804, L≈3%) → ALL 8 tones between L=1.5% and L=4% (useless — everything near-black)
- Mid-dark base (#1a3a12, L≈15%) → cool tones 7-22% (still all dark, no mid-range)
- With #206f20 (L=28%) → cool ramp L=12.6%–36.4%
- With #90df90 (L=72%) → warm ramp L=32.4%–93.6%
- Combined: L=12.6% to L=93.6% = 81% range with overlap at 32-36%

**This explains every previous "palette is too narrow" failure:**
Previous iterations used near-black bases (#020804, #081008) thinking "dark green = near-black tones."
But the engine multiplies the ALREADY-dark lightness, making the ramp even narrower.
For maximal range: base colors should be at L=25-30% (dark) and L=70-75% (bright).

### NEW TRANSFERABLE DISCOVERIES:

1. **PALETTE BASE LIGHTNESS DETERMINES RANGE**
   - The engine's ColorRamp scales lightness: deepShadow=L*0.45, shadow=L*0.70, highlight=L*1.30
   - Near-black/near-white bases produce NARROW ramps (all tones cluster near the base)
   - For maximum range: use base at L=25-30% (8 tones spanning L=12-36%) and L=70-75% (8 tones spanning L=32-94%)
   - Total coverage: 12-94% luminance with smooth overlap in the mid-range
   - Applies to: any palette-based rendering — ALWAYS verify the actual tones before iterating on rendering code

2. **HUE SHIFT IN RAMPS: +20° SHADOW, -15° HIGHLIGHT**
   - Engine shifts hue cooler in shadows (H+20°), warmer in highlights (H-15°)
   - This is automatic — don't fight it. Choose base hues knowing this shift will happen.
   - For green (H=120°): shadows shift toward cyan (140°), highlights toward yellow-green (105°)
   - Applies to: any colour planning for palette-based sprites

3. **PALETTE DIAGNOSTIC BEFORE RENDERING**
   - ALWAYS render a flat gradient strip of all palette tones before doing ANY drawing work
   - Verify: darkest tone reads near-black, brightest near-white, no large gaps
   - This takes 2 minutes and prevents hours of futile iteration on rendering code
   - Applies to: any new sprite project

4. **MICRO-DOMES (4-5px) > DOT PAIRS (2px) FOR TEXTURE**
   - 2px highlight+shadow dot pairs are too small to read as convex forms — they look like noise
   - 4-5px domes with dome normals computed per-pixel create visible bumps
   - Blend 50% global position lighting + 50% local bump lighting
   - Still scored 2/5 but visibly better than previous attempts
   - Applies to: any surface texture that needs to read at small sizes

### NEW TRANSFERABLE DISCOVERIES:

1. **VORONOI TESSELLATION FOR PACKED TEXTURE**
   - Assign every pixel to its nearest feature center (Voronoi cell)
   - Crevice at cell boundary (where distance to nearest ≈ distance to second-nearest)
   - This guarantees 100% coverage by construction — no base gradient visible anywhere
   - Each cell is independently lit with sphere normals from its center
   - The boundary threshold controls crevice width: 0.12-0.13 ≈ 1-2px dark lines
   - Applies to: any densely packed surface (scales, cobblestones, tiles, chainmail, bark)

2. **HARDCODED EXTREMES AT ALL BOUNDARIES BACKFIRES**
   - Forcing the absolute brightest tone at EVERY ridge creates a uniform white grid
   - This reads as MORE artificial than computed values (violates organic asymmetry)
   - Better: ridges should be the LOCALLY brightest element (varies by global position)
   - Shadow-zone ridges at 50-60% look natural; highlight-zone ridges at 90-95% look natural
   - ONLY use hardcoded extremes for crevices (DARKEST) and specular (BRIGHTEST)
   - Applies to: any edge pair rendering — extremes should be used selectively, not everywhere

3. **DOME RADIUS FOR IDENTIFIABILITY: 5-7px MINIMUM**
   - r=3-5 domes blend into noise at 128x128 — too small to show individual highlight/shadow
   - r=5-7 domes are large enough for per-pixel sphere lighting to create visible tonal sweep
   - With Voronoi at spacing 9, r=5.5-7 gives cells of ~9-14px diameter — individually identifiable
   - Applies to: any repeated micro-feature — below 5px, features merge into texture noise

4. **HEIGHT FIELD ≠ GOOD TEXTURE**
   - Computing bumps as a height field and deriving normals produces continuous, smooth variations
   - These read as noise/dithering, not as individually distinct 3D forms
   - Explicit features (Voronoi cells) with per-feature normals produce identifiable forms
   - The difference: height field has gradual transitions, Voronoi has sharp boundaries
   - Applies to: any organic surface — explicit features > procedural height modulation

5. **GLOBAL MODULATION AS MULTIPLIER, NOT BLEND**
   - Blending global + local lighting (e.g., 40% global + 60% local) compresses per-feature range
   - Multiplicative modulation (local * (0.25 + global * 0.75)) preserves per-feature contrast while varying overall brightness
   - This means shadow-zone features are darker overall but still have internal contrast
   - Applies to: any scene combining global and local lighting

## Iteration 7 (exercise 1, attempts 28-32) — 2026-03-28

### Result: FAIL after 5 attempts. Texture steady at 3/5, Tonal Range/Edges/Specular still 2/5.

### NEW TRANSFERABLE DISCOVERIES:

1. **PALETTE TONE DISTRIBUTION vs RANGE**
   - Having extreme hex values doesn't guarantee visible tonal range
   - A cool base at L=12% produces 8 tones between L=5-16% — ALL look the same dark green
   - A cool base at L=20% produces 8 tones between L=9-26% — each is visually distinct
   - The DISTRIBUTION across tones matters more than the absolute extremes
   - Optimal bases: L=20-25% (cool) and L=72-76% (warm) for 16 visually distinct tones
   - Applies to: any palette-based rendering — verify tone DISTRIBUTION, not just range endpoints

2. **GAMMA CURVE vs S-CURVE FOR PER-FEATURE RENDERING**
   - S-curve pushes extremes apart but creates a UNIFORM DARK BLOB on shadow side
   - At NdotL=0 (30% of sphere surface), S-curve produces v=0 for ALL those pixels
   - Gamma 0.65 distributes tones more evenly — the shadow side shows gradation
   - S-curve is better for overall composition; gamma is better for per-feature detail
   - Applies to: any repeated form where you need visible tonal sweep within each form

3. **SPECULAR PLACEMENT PARADOX — MID-TONE ZONES**
   - On bright surfaces (L=80%+): specular at L=99% has only 19% contrast — invisible
   - On dark surfaces (L=10-20%): specular at L=99% has 80%+ contrast BUT 2-3px is too small to see against dark expanse
   - The sweet spot should be MID-TONE zones (L=40-50%): high contrast (50%+) AND visible against medium background
   - ALSO: specular clusters need to be 4-5px, not 2-3px, to be identifiable at 128x128
   - Applies to: any specular highlight placement

4. **BOTH MULTIPLICATIVE AND ADDITIVE GLOBAL BLEND COMPRESS PER-FEATURE RANGE**
   - Multiplicative (v * globalMod): shadow-zone features compressed to 0-20%
   - Additive (v * 0.82 + gl * 0.18): highlight-zone features lifted floor to 15%+
   - Neither preserves the full 0-100% range per feature
   - For maximum per-feature range: NO global modulation on feature interiors
   - Use global modulation ONLY on boundaries (ridge brightness, crevice depth)
   - Applies to: any scene combining global form lighting with local detail

5. **16-TONE RAMP MUST BE MONOTONIC**
   - With 2 palette groups, the combined 16-tone ramp is cool[0..7] + warm[0..7]

### KEY NEW TECHNIQUE: VALUE HIERARCHY VIA TONAL WINDOW ASSIGNMENT
- The exercise required scales to RECEDE and iris to be BRIGHTEST
- Solution: assign different tonal ceilings per material layer
- Scales: gb * 6 (max base index 6) → tones cap at ~13 → never reach near-white
- Scale ridges capped at index 13 (not 15) → bright but not brightest
- Iris: full palette 0-15 → brightest tones reserved for focal element
- Scale specular: FEWER clusters (max 3), SMALLER (3px), using SCALE_SECOND not SCALE_BRIGHT
- Iris specular: LARGER (8px core), using SCALE_BRIGHT (near-white) → single brightest point
- The hierarchy is: iris specular > iris fibres > lid rim > scale ridges > scale interiors

### NEW TRANSFERABLE DISCOVERIES:

1. **VALUE HIERARCHY VIA TONAL WINDOW CAPPING**
   - When compositing multiple materials, assign different tonal CEILINGS per layer
   - Background elements: cap at palette 13/15 (never reach near-white)
   - Focal elements: full range 0-15 (allowed to reach extremes)
   - The eye naturally goes to the HIGHEST CONTRAST area
   - Implementation: reduce the gb multiplier (gb * 6 vs gb * 9) and cap zone indices
   - Applies to: any scene with foreground/background separation — hero characters vs environment, UI highlights vs base elements

2. **SPECULAR QUANTITY INVERSELY PROPORTIONAL TO IMPORTANCE**
   - Background scales: 2-3 small speculars max (recede)
   - Focal element (iris): 2 large speculars (dominate)
   - The iris specular must be the SINGLE brightest point in the entire image
   - Scale speculars should be noticeably dimmer (use SCALE_SECOND not SCALE_BRIGHT)
   - Applies to: any scene with specular highlights — fewer but brighter on the focal point

3. **WIDER CANVAS IMPROVES COMPOSITION DRAMATICALLY**
   - 192x128 (3:2) provides much more room for scales to fill naturally vs 128x128 (1:1)
   - The eye feels naturally embedded in a scaled surface, not squeezed into a square
   - More peripheral scales = more context = more immersive composition
   - The wider format also matches the reference images better (all are landscape)
   - Applies to: composition framing — match canvas aspect ratio to subject shape

4. **ALL PREVIOUS TECHNIQUES COMPOSE WITHOUT MODIFICATION**
   - 15 iterations of technique development composed cleanly into the most complex piece yet
   - No technique needed modification — only parameter tuning (tonal windows, specular sizes)
   - This confirms the training loop is producing genuinely transferable, composable skills
   - The composition itself IS the new technique — the parts are all proven

### Critic feedback for future exercises:

## Iteration 18 (exercise 9, attempt 1) — 2026-03-29

### Result: PASS on first attempt! Ninth consecutive first-attempt pass.

### Critic scores: Texture 3, Tonal Range 4, Edges 4, Specular 3, Coverage 4, Colour 3, Construction 4, Craft 3

### What worked:
- Third consecutive palette-only transposition — same rendering code as ex7/ex8, only 4 hex values changed
- Iridescent scales rendered via SATURATION GRADIENT in palette: cool group = deep saturated blue-purple, warm group = desaturated silver-blue
- Purple iris (irisDeep: '#381870') with golden centre (irisBright: '#d8a838') — strong warm-on-cool focal contrast

- All value hierarchy logic from ex7/ex8 transferred without modification

### NEW TRANSFERABLE DISCOVERIES:

1. **IRIDESCENCE VIA PALETTE SATURATION GRADIENT**
   - Iridescence = colour changes with viewing angle/surface normal
   - Implementation: one palette group for HIGH SATURATION (shadow/oblique normals), another for LOW SATURATION (lit/facing normals)
   - The palette transition itself creates the hue+saturation shift that reads as iridescence
   - Cool (shadow): deep saturated blue-purple (#182058, H≈225°, high S)
   - Warm (highlight): desaturated silver-blue (#a8b8d8, H≈215°, low S)
   - The zone-based rendering + range-band shifting naturally blends between these groups
   - Applies to: beetle shells, oil slicks, metallic finishes, magic effects, soap bubbles

2. **ENGINE HUE SHIFT AS IRIDESCENCE ALLY ON COOL PALETTES**
   - Engine shifts shadows +20° and highlights -15°
   - For blue (H≈225°): shadows → H≈245° (purple), highlights → H≈210° (cyan-silver)
   - This blue→purple→silver shift IS natural iridescence — engine does it for free
   - Unlike warm palettes where the shift fights the target colour, cool palettes benefit
   - Applies to: any blue/purple/teal iridescent surface — the engine's hue shift is an ally, not an enemy

3. **MONOCHROMATIC RISK WITH COOL DESATURATED PALETTES**
   - The critic flagged "reads as blue-grey rather than assertively blue"
   - Desaturated silver warm group (#a8b8d8) blends with saturated blue cool group (#182058), but the silver doesn't pop enough
   - The issue: silver/grey is inherently ambiguous — it could be "blue-grey" or just "grey"
   - Fix for future: push warm group more toward TRUE SILVER (even lower saturation, higher lightness) while keeping cool group deeply saturated
   - The saturation GAP between groups determines iridescent drama — wider gap = more dramatic
   - Applies to: any iridescent palette — the saturation difference between groups determines perceived colour shift intensity

### Critic notes for future:
- Push silver highlights even more desaturated/bright for stronger iridescent contrast
- Iris specular reads as blocky rectangle — use asymmetric cluster for wet glint
- Scale speculars blend into highlight zones — need darker surrounding context
- Overall colour assertiveness lower than ex8 — blue-grey less visually bold than red
