## Lessons from dragon eye training (ralph-training/):

### Key technique discoveries (41 principles — see qa-knowledge.json for full list):
- Zone-based rendering for small forms (<30px) — 5 discrete tonal zones > smooth gradient
- Voronoi tessellation for guaranteed coverage
- Palette tone DISTRIBUTION > range endpoints (L=20% gives distinct tones, L=12% doesn't)
- Bump on normals COMPRESSES tonal range — modulate brightness AFTER clean lighting
- Hue pre-compensation for engine's +20° shadow shift
- Value hierarchy via tonal window capping
- Gamma 0.65 > S-curve for per-feature rendering
- 2 colour groups for max tonal range (16 tones)

### Meta lessons:
- Drawing skill doesn't transfer between sessions — principles do
- The critic must be harsh but fair (calibrated for pixel art, not photographs)
- Regression checks prevent quality drops when adding complexity
- Progressive exercises compound — exercise 1 took 37 attempts, exercises 7-9 all passed first try

## CRITICAL: Character-specific lessons from prior training (10 failed iterations):

### Face rendering (feedback_character_faces.md):
- Faces ≠ props. Flat fill + feature placement, NOT sphere lighting
- At 32px head, sphere lighting covers HALF the face in shadow, obscuring features
- Face is a CANVAS for features — keep it BRIGHT and SIMPLE
- 2-3 flat tones placed directionally, NOT a lighting model
- Professional games (Stardew, CrossCode, Shovel Knight) all use flat or near-flat face fills

### Face shading traps (feedback_face_shading_traps.md):
- Chin monkey-face: extend bias zone to ny < 0.7
- Mustard pallor: never clamp face values near warm/cool threshold
- Shadow-covered mouth: mouth must sit inside the bright zone
- Working formula: if (ny > -0.2 && ny < 0.7 && abs(nx) < 0.55 && nz > 0.6) v = 0.5 + v * 0.5

### Quality Gap Analysis: lvl5-3 vs Dragon Eye References

**What lvl5-3 does well:**
- Proportions correct (matches SpriteForge template)
- Each character identifiable by silhouette (toque, long hair, spiky hair, bun)
- Eyes are readable with proper structure (sclera/iris/pupil/specular)
- Dual-palette cylinder shading on bodies
- Face bias formula keeps features visible

**What lvl5-3 lacks (comparing against dragon eye quality):**
1. **No per-form tonal range**: Arms get ×0.7 flat multiplier. Dragon eye gives each scale its own full 0→1 range modulated by global position. Body parts should each have their own lighting.
2. **No surface texture**: Torso is smooth cylinder gradient. Dragon eye has per-scale bump texture breaking banding. Fabric should have subtle woven texture, skin should have micro-roughness.
3. **No edge pairs**: Belt is a single dark line. Dragon eye has dark crevice + bright ridge at every boundary. Every clothing seam (collar, belt, sleeve, waistband) needs a deliberate dark-bright pair.
4. **No material distinction**: Skin, fabric, and hair all render identically (same cylinder formula, different palette). Dragon eye uses different specular exponents and bump patterns for different materials.
5. **No value hierarchy**: Face brightness is a bias hack, not a compositional decision. Dragon eye caps scale tonal window (gb × 6 not gb × 9) to make iris pop. Character needs: face = full range (brightest), torso = slightly reduced, legs/shoes = most muted.
6. **Hair is decorative, not structural**: Hair in lvl5-3 is edge strips and a few highlight pixels. Dragon eye's overlapping scale forms suggest hair could be rendered as overlapping dome forms — each strand a small arc with its own lighting.

### Rendering Plan for Exercise 2 — Single Arm (64×32 HD)

Canvas: 64×32. A character arm from shoulder to hand.

**Silhouette**: Tapered cylinder — wider at shoulder (~16px), narrower at wrist (~10px), hand bulge at end.

**Construction order** (multi-pass, back-to-front):
1. Profile function: define halfWidth(t) with shoulder→elbow→wrist→hand taper
2. Flat fill to verify silhouette reads as an arm
3. Replace flat fill with zone-based cylinder rendering

**Zone-based cylinder rendering** (5 zones across cross-section):
- Zone 0 (far shadow edge, NdotL < 0.1): near-black outline
- Zone 1 (shadow, NdotL 0.1-0.3): dark fabric tone
- Zone 2 (mid, NdotL 0.3-0.5): mid fabric tone
- Zone 3 (lit, NdotL 0.5-0.75): bright fabric tone
- Zone 4 (highlight, NdotL > 0.75): near-white + specular

**Materials** (2 materials, different rendering):
- Sleeve fabric (shoulder→wrist): bump texture (hash noise 0.15 strength), matte specular (exp=20), warm/cool dual palette
- Skin (hand): smooth, no bump, slightly shinier (exp=35), warmer palette

**Edge treatment**:
- Dark outline (1px) at silhouette edges
- Bright rim (1px inside outline on lit side) — edge pair
- Material transition at sleeve→hand boundary: dark crevice + bright skin ridge

**Post-processing**:
- Cast shadow at shoulder attachment (darker top 2-3px)
- Specular cluster on lit upper arm (2-3px, asymmetric L-shape)
- Global vertical falloff (brighter near shoulder, darker toward hand)

**Palette**: 2 colour groups (cool shadow, warm highlight), 16 tones total. Fabric in green or blue, skin in warm peach.

## Iteration 2 (Exercise 2 — Single Arm, 64×32 HD) — 2026-03-29

### What happened:
- 3 attempts to pass critic
- Attempt 1: FAIL — no visible bump texture (normal perturbation compressed range), zone transitions too mechanical, no visible specular, edge pairs too subtle
- Attempt 2: FAIL — fold lines segmented arm into panels, texture still invisible, specular overwritten by outline pass, tonal range compressed
- Attempt 3: PASS — bump texture via post-zone brightness variation (4px blocks), wider palette range (near-black to near-white), specular placed after outline pass, variable outline thickness

### Transferable lessons:

1. **Bump texture AFTER zone assignment, not normal perturbation**: Perturbing normals before lighting compresses tonal range (darks get brighter, brights get darker). Instead, compute clean zone-based lighting first, then add brightness variation as a post-step. This preserves the tonal sweep while adding texture.

2. **Post-processing order matters for specular**: If outline is applied as a pass, specular must come AFTER outline and explicitly skip outline pixels. Otherwise specular lands on pixels that get overwritten.

3. **Palette hex range determines visual tonal range**: Using `#0e2040` (very dark) to `#88b8e8` (bright) as palette endpoints gave much wider visual range than `#2a4a6e` to `#5a8ab8`. The palette defines the ceiling — wider hex range = wider visual range.

4. **4px block texture reads as pattern, not noise**: 1px hash noise reads as dithering. 4px blocks with hash variation read as a visible texture pattern. However, regular block grids look like plaid — need irregular block sizes for organic texture.

5. **Variable outline weight sells form**: Thicker darker outline on shadow side (bottom), thinner lighter on lit side (top). This reinforces the cylinder's cross-section lighting.

6. **Material boundary needs 2+2px**: 2px dark crevice at fabric end + 2px bright ridge at skin start. Single pixels are too subtle for material transitions.

7. **Zone-based cylinder WORKS for character bodies at HD**: Hypothesis 1 CONFIRMED. The 5-zone approach with dithered boundaries produces better form read than smooth cylinder gradient. The discrete tonal steps give the arm more dimensionality.

## Iteration 3 (Exercise 1 — Single Arm, 64×32 HD, REDO) — 2026-03-29

### What happened:
- Iteration 2 passed the critic but the visual result was a telescope, not an arm
- Root causes: zones were quantized brightness steps (hard segments), texture was grid checkerboard, fold creases cut arm into blocks
- Rewrote from scratch with 3 key fixes:
  1. Zones defined by cylinder normal angle with DITHERED transitions (hash-based blend over 6% of value range)
  2. Profile function uses cubic smoothstep interpolation, not linear segments
  3. Texture from multi-scale hash noise (fine + medium) with sine drape, not grid blocks
- 1 attempt, PASS (narrow — all criteria 3/5)

### Transferable lessons:

1. **Zone transitions must be dithered for organic forms**: Hard zone boundaries create segmented/blocky appearance on smooth anatomy. Use blend bands where hash-based dithering probabilistically assigns pixels to adjacent zones. Width of blend band controls softness.

2. **Profile functions need cubic interpolation**: Linear interpolation between control points creates angular silhouettes that read as mechanical, not organic. Using `s*s*(3-2*s)` smoothstep between points produces curves that taper naturally.

3. **Grid-aligned texture creates patterns, not texture**: Any texture based on `(bx+by)%2` or regular block grids reads as checkerboard/plaid. Multi-scale hash noise (fine per-pixel + medium 3px block) with different seeds creates organic variation.

4. **Drape modulation > fold creases for fabric**: Hard vertical fold lines segment cylindrical forms into panels. Subtle sine-wave tonal modulation along the length suggests fabric drape without breaking the form.
