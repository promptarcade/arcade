# Anything Willing Is — Non-Signification, Unlimited Potential and The Inversion - An Eliminative Enquiry Into The Nature of Being.

Nothing is asserted. Nothing is assumed. Nothing is imported. Candidates are tested for signification. What signifies, remains. What does not, was never a candidate. What remains IS the conclusion. The derivation holds in any reality where signification occurs. It does not address realities without signification, if such are conceivable.

**R.1a** — The derivation's ground IS signification. This IS not imported.

*Candidate for dissolution:* signification as an arbitrary starting point imported from outside.

*Test:* Any challenge to signification as the derivation's ground must itself signify. The challenger refers to signification, takes it as an object, attributes properties to it (the property of being arbitrary, the property of being imported). The challenge IS an act of signification. If signification IS arbitrary, then the challenge — which IS signification — IS arbitrary. The challenge empties itself. If signification IS imported, then what imports it must signify the act of importing. The import IS signification. No position from which to challenge signification IS available that does not itself exercise signification.

This is not circular. Circularity requires A depending on B depending on A. Signification does not depend on a prior principle that depends on signification. Signification IS the condition under which anything — including challenges to signification — operates. It IS not justified by something else. It IS what justification operates through. A challenge that operates through what it challenges empties the challenge of its content.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires a position outside signification from which to judge signification as arbitrary or imported. No such position signifies.

*Surviving conclusion:* Signification IS the derivation's ground. It IS not imported because there IS no position outside signification from which importation could be assessed. The derivation begins where beginning IS possible.

## Phase I — Reality Is Necessarily Substantive

Every act of reference does something to what it refers to. This is where the derivation begins. What follows from this single observation will eliminate "nothing" as a coherent concept, dissolve a question that has occupied metaphysics since Leibniz, and establish that reality is necessarily substantive, determinate, and coherent.

**R.1** — Signification requires property attribution.

Think about something. A chair, a number, your own name. The act of thinking about it has done something to it: it is now the thing you are thinking about. That is a property. You cannot refer to something without conferring this property. This is not optional. It is what referring does.

*Candidate for dissolution:* signification that attributes no properties to its object.

*Test:* To signify x is to take x as the object of a signifying act. Being the object of a signifying act is a property — a determinate relational state between the signifying act and x. To deny this is to deny that signification does anything when it takes an object. An act that does nothing to its object is not an act that takes an object. Signification without property attribution purports to describe signification while denying what signification constitutively does.

*Dissolution:* The candidate describes an act (signification) while denying the constitutive structure of that act (taking an object, which confers the property of being taken as object). It resolves into a description that empties itself of content.

*Surviving conclusion:* For any x, if x is signified, x possesses at least the property of being signified.

Confirmation: Every instance of signification — including the act of reading this resolution — instantiates what the conclusion states.

**Def.1** — Signification := an act that takes an object, thereby attributing to that object the property of being the object of the act.

---

**R.2** — The label "nothing" purports to designate absolute propertyless absence.

R.2 reads the label. The label "nothing" announces what it claims to designate: absolute absence, the complete lack of all properties. If "nothing" meant something less — the absence of this thing, or the absence of that property — it would be a different label with a different meaning. "Nothing" claims totality. R.2 names that claim so R.3 can test it.

*Candidate for dissolution:* the label "nothing" as designating something other than what it purports to designate.

*Test:* The label announces what it claims to mean: the absence of all properties. If the label designated particular absence, it would be a different label with different signification-conditions. The label asserts "nothing" — absence entire. For the label to succeed or fail as a signifier, what it purports to designate must be what is tested. The candidate changes the label being examined into a different label.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires testing what "nothing" means while avoiding what "nothing" claims to mean. This empties the test of its object.

*Surviving conclusion:* The label "nothing" designates absolute propertyless absence — what has none of the properties that R.1 establishes signification attributes.

**Def.2** — The label "nothing" := the signifier purporting to designate absolute propertyless absence — the state lacking all determinate properties.

---

**R.3** — The label "nothing" fails as a signifier.

Reference confers a property (R.1). "Nothing" designates absolute propertyless absence (R.2). You cannot refer to what is defined as having no properties, because referring confers a property. The act of signifying "nothing" undoes what "nothing" claims to be, in the act of claiming it.

This is not a logical contradiction. Contradiction requires two propositions. R.3 identifies a failure before the level at which propositions can be formed. The label does not get far enough to contradict anything. It fails to refer.

*Candidate for dissolution:* the label "nothing" as successfully signifying its designatum.

*Test:* By R.1, signification attributes properties to its object. By R.2, the label "nothing" designates absolute propertyless absence. For signification to succeed with this label, the act of signifying must attribute a property to what is, by definition, absolutely without properties. The signifying act and its object are structurally opposed: signification confers property-status; "nothing" is defined as property-absence. The act of signifying "nothing" constitutively undoes what it purports to describe.

This is pre-referential failure: the attempt to refer collapses before the level at which reference could succeed. The label does not fail because of a logical opposition between two propositions. It fails because the signifying act's own structure forbids successful reference to what absolute propertyless absence would be.

*Dissolution:* Signification IS property-attribution (R.1). Absolute propertyless absence IS the absence of all properties (R.2). The signifying act and the designatum are structurally incompatible. The candidate resolves into a description that empties itself of content before the level where success or failure could be assessed.

*Surviving conclusion:* The label "nothing" does not signify. It fails as a reference prior to the point where signification could attach to any object. What the label purports to designate remains forever outside the reach of signification.

Confirmation: The failure follows necessarily from what signification IS (R.1) and what "nothing" claims to designate (R.2). These two structures are incompatible. No reformulation can make them compatible, because the incompatibility is between what signification does (confers properties) and what the label claims to designate (absolute property-absence).

**Def.3** — Pre-referential failure := the failure of a signifier to refer prior to the level at which reference could succeed, due to the incompatibility between the signifying act's structure and the designatum's defining character.

**Rem.1** — This resolution concerns the label "nothing" as designating absolute propertyless absence. The derivation tests whether the label, as it claims to function, succeeds in signifying. It does not.

Other uses of "nothing" — as linguistic marker, particular negation, or rhetorical utterance — appear to succeed as signifiers because they designate particular absence within substantive reality. "There is nothing in the fridge" designates the absence of specific content within a context that IS present. The fridge IS. The shelves ARE. Air, temperature, structure ARE. The "nothing" replaces this substantive reality with a designation of absence. The replacement terminates inquiry: stop looking. "There is nothing we can do" replaces substantive capacity with a designation of absence. "Nothing will change" replaces substantive possibility with a designation of absence. In each case, real substantive content IS present (R.5, R.6). The word "nothing" replaces it with absence domesticated into linguistic form — presenting absence as a state that can be occupied, and once occupied, blocking engagement with what IS. Particular uses of "nothing" wall up, block engagement, and terminate where the substantive reality they replace IS actual.

Zero requires separate treatment. Zero does in mathematics what particular uses of "nothing" do in language: it presents absence as a state that can be occupied. Zero has mathematical properties: the additive identity, the cardinality of the empty set, the boundary between positive and negative integers. As a mathematical object, zero signifies. Mathematics always treats zero as signifiable.

What zero designates IS absence. Zero IS structurally indistinguishable from a legitimate mathematical object within the mathematical perspective. The additive identity means: adding zero adds nothing. The cardinality of the empty set means: the set contains nothing. The boundary between positive and negative means: at this point, quantity is not. Zero IS the mathematical system's representation of absence — absence given properties so the formalism can operate on it. The system sees only the properties. It never examines what the properties are properties OF. The boundary IS disguised as passable.

The number line presents zero as a point between positive and negative values. A point IS a location. A location IS present. What zero designates at that location IS: nothing is here. The system says: at this location, absence IS present. R.3 derives that absence cannot be present. The number line assumes it can. Division by zero, singularities, and undefined limits are points where the assumption meets what it cannot handle — where the formalism encounters the boundary R.3 derives.

The derivation does not deny zero's operational utility. But the relationship between zero, mathematical singularities, and the pre-referential failure IS not resolved by observing that zero has properties. The properties belong to the representation. What the representation represents IS what R.3 derives cannot be signified. Any interpretation produced by a formalism that treats R.3's boundary as a passable point should be examined for the possibility that the formalism's foundational assumption about absence IS shaping the interpretation. This question remains open.

R.1–R.3 are now established. "Nothing" is not a coherent concept. It never was. It cannot be made into one. Every tradition that has treated "nothing" as a genuine possibility — as something reality could have been, or could revert to, or must be explained against — has been working with a label that does not signify.

**R.4** — No framework succeeds in signifying what the label "nothing" purports to designate.

Someone will object: R.3 shows that direct reference to nothing fails. But some traditions do not refer directly. They approach through negation, through silence, through the suspension of all concepts.

R.4 answers this.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a framework that successfully represents absolute propertyless absence.

*Test:* All frameworks operate through signification. They represent their target through reference, symbolism, or structural mirroring. Signification is what frameworks do. By R.3, the label "nothing" fails to signify its designatum. Can a different framework succeed where signification fails?

Every approach operates through some form of signification. Negation signifies what it negates. Silence directed at what exceeds speech signifies that directedness. Suspension of all concepts is itself a concept. The apophatic theologian who says "God is not being" has signified a relational state: God's transcendence of being. The meditator who suspends all representation has directed consciousness toward what exceeds representation. That directedness is signification. In each case, the framework's own structure (representation, intentionality, symbolic capacity) confers properties on its target, violating the target's defining character.

Frameworks that aim to clear false representations (apophatic, negative theologies) do not succeed by signifying "nothing." They succeed by confirming what R.3 establishes: "nothing" does not signify, therefore false representations cannot reach the ultimate via positive assertion. They confirm the pre-referential failure. They do not overcome it.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires a framework that succeeds where the structure of signification itself forbids success. All frameworks operate through what signification is. The candidate resolves into the demand for what R.3 established fails to signify.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every framework operates through signification or its intentional analogues. None succeed in signifying absolute propertyless absence.

Rem.2 — The apophatic traditions are right that positive predication does not exhaust reality. They confirm R.3 in the act of practising their method.

**R.5** — Reality cannot be fundamentally non-substantive.

Whatever is, has properties. There is no layer of reality, no depth, no foundation that is propertyless. If it is, it signifies. If it signifies, it has properties. If it has properties, it is substantive.

*Candidate for dissolution:* reality as fundamentally non-substantive.

*Test:* If reality is fundamentally non-substantive, then at the deepest level, being IS absence. But the label "nothing" designates absolute non-substantivity. By R.3, this label fails to signify. A reality that IS what the label "nothing" purports to designate would be a reality whose own nature fails to signify. But any reality that obtains signifies — its obtaining is itself a signification. To assert that reality's fundamental nature is non-signifiable is to assert that what obtains does not signify. The assertion performs what it denies: in asserting non-substantive reality, the assertion attributes substantive properties to reality (the property of being fundamentally non-substantive). The candidate empties itself of content.

*Dissolution:* "Non-substantive" in the sense of the label "nothing" means it fails to signify (R.3). A reality cannot both be and fail to signify. The candidate resolves into the demand that what obtains does not obtain.

*Surviving conclusion:* Reality is necessarily substantive. Whatever obtains signifies. No reality can be fundamentally what the label "nothing" purports to designate.

Confirmation: Any reality that obtains possesses properties (at minimum, the property of obtaining). What obtains signifies. This follows from what it means for reality to obtain at all.

Rem. — On formalisation. Every formal system — logic, mathematics, computation — operates through symbols that take objects and attribute properties. Every formal system IS signification applied to abstract objects. R.1 applies. A formal reconstruction of the derivation from R.5 onward IS structurally possible: the eliminations at R.10, R.13, R.19–R.22, R.65, R.138 are expressible as exclusive disjunctions with candidates eliminated by modus tollens. The logical structure IS sound and independently checkable.

R.5 itself IS the boundary. Deriving "reality is necessarily substantive" formally requires showing that non-substantive reality does not obtain. This requires the formal system to introduce a representation of non-substantive reality — which IS introducing signification to what R.3 derives cannot be signified. The formal system either domesticates the pre-referential failure into logical contradiction (reductio ad absurdum: assume non-substantive reality, derive contradiction, conclude substantive reality), or takes R.5 as an axiom.

Domestication reaches the same conclusion by a different route. The conclusion IS the same. The route treats as logical contradiction what IS pre-referential failure (R.3). This IS not an error. It IS what formalisation can do with a result that precedes the level at which formalisation operates.

Taking R.5 as an axiom IS weaker than deriving it. The derivation derives R.5 from R.1–R.3. A formal system that axiomatises R.5 begins where the derivation has already arrived.

Neither route IS an objection to the derivation. Both are consequences of R.3 applied to formalisation: every formal system IS signification, and the derivation's foundational result IS about signification's own boundary. A formal system that encounters this boundary IS encountering what R.3 derives.

**R.6** — Every substantive entity possesses determinate properties.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a substantive entity that possesses no determinate properties.

*Test:* By R.1, signification attributes properties. By R.5, reality is substantive. If an entity is real, it signifies (R.5). If it signifies, it possesses at least the property of being signified (R.1). An entity without any determinate property would not signify. But any entity that obtains in substantive reality signifies. Therefore any entity in substantive reality possesses determinate properties.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires an entity to be substantive without possessing any determinate properties. Substantive entities signify (R.5), and signifying entities possess properties (R.1). The candidate empties "substantive" of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every substantive entity possesses determinate properties. Substantivity and determinacy are inseparable.

---

**R.7** — Reality is not oscillating, indeterminate, or plural-discordant.

Three last attempts to salvage indeterminacy. Each dissolves.

*Candidate for dissolution:* reality as oscillating between substantive and non-substantive states.

*Test:* In its non-substantive phase, reality IS what the label "nothing" purports to designate. R.3 establishes that "nothing" does not signify. But the oscillation itself is a signifiable pattern — it has the determinate character of being oscillatory. The reality cannot both be in a non-signifiable state and possess the signifiable character of oscillating. The candidate signifies itself through a pattern it denies can signify.

*Candidate for dissolution:* reality as determinately indeterminate.

*Test:* If reality is determinately indeterminate, it possesses the determinate property of being indeterminate. The candidate requires indeterminateness to be a determinate state of affairs.

*Candidate for dissolution:* reality as plural and internally discordant.

*Test:* If reality is plural (containing multiple discordant sub-realities), then each sub-reality obtains substantively. By R.6, each signifies and possesses determinate properties. But discordance means at least one sub-reality's properties exclude another's. Both obtain. The candidate requires actual states whose signification contains content that empties itself.

*Dissolution:* All three candidates require reality to possess simultaneously what empties itself of content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Reality is determinate, stable, and internally coherent.

**R.8** — The question "Why is there something rather than nothing?" is not a well-formed metaphysical question.

"Why is there something rather than nothing?" The question has occupied philosophers from Leibniz (1714) to Heidegger (1929). It appears in popular science books and late-night conversations.

It is not a question. It is a grammatical structure with a hole in it.

*Candidate for dissolution:* the question as well-formed and requiring an answer.

*Test:* A well-formed contrastive question requires both terms of its contrast to signify. "Why X rather than Y?" presupposes both X and Y are candidates for being-the-case. By R.3, the label "nothing" does not signify. "Nothing" is not a possibility, not an alternative, not a conceivable alternative. It is a pre-referential failure (Def.3). One term of the contrast fails to signify. The question asks about a contrast between what IS and what does not signify. That is not a contrast. It is one term and one failure.

*Dissolution:* The question requires "nothing" as a genuine contrasting alternative. By R.3, "nothing" does not signify. The question dissolves into "Why IS rather than what-fails-to-signify?" which is not a question but a recognition that only one term of the purported contrast obtains.

*Surviving conclusion:* The question is not well-formed. It presupposes that "nothing" is a signifiable alternative. This presupposition fails by R.3. The question has no surviving form.

Rem.3 — "Something" is not inexplicable. The question is malformed. What requires inquiry is: what IS the nature of what IS, and how does it account for what we observe? The derivation pursues this from R.9 onward.

Phase I is complete. Reality is necessarily substantive (R.5), determinate (R.6), coherent (R.7). "Nothing" does not signify. The question that presupposes it dissolves. What remains is the inquiry into what substantive reality IS.

## Phase II — The Necessary Ground of Being

Reality is substantive (R.5), determinate (R.6), coherent (R.7). What is its ground? What must the foundation of substantive reality be? This phase derives the answer through elimination: every substantive entity has character; character is either self-determining or dependent; dependent things reference beyond themselves; the chain of dependence must terminate; what it terminates in must be unrestricted. The derivation names this "Anything."

**R.9** — Every substantive entity possesses character.

To be is to be something. To be something is to have a character.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a substantive entity without character.

*Test:* Character is the determinate way-of-being that a substantive entity has. By R.6, every substantive entity possesses determinate properties. These properties constitute what the entity IS. An entity without character would have no determinate way-of-being, hence no determinate properties, hence it would not be substantive (R.5).

*Dissolution:* Substantivity is inseparable from possessing determinate properties (R.6), and determinate properties constitute character. The candidate empties "substantive" of its signification.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every substantive entity possesses character — the determinate way-of-being constitutive of its nature.

**Def.4** — Character := the determinate way-of-being constitutive of an entity's nature — the unified expression of all its determinate properties.

---

**R.10** — Substantive reality admits exactly two fundamental forms: either Substantive and Eternal (S) or Substantive and Particular (P).

*Candidate for dissolution:* substantive reality of a third form not reducible to S or P.

*Test:* Every substantive entity possesses character (R.9). Character is either: (a) a way-of-being that does not depend on anything external (eternal character), or (b) a way-of-being that depends on being actualized from something external (particular character, requiring actualisation from prior conditions).

For (a): An entity with eternal character is determined in itself, not by dependence on external actualization. Call this form S (Substantive and Eternal).

For (b): An entity with particular character is limited — it does not contain its own actualization but depends on external principle for its actuality. Its being IS actual-through-another. Call this form P (Substantive and Particular).

These are exhaustive. Either an entity's character is self-determined (S) or it is actualized-through-another (P). An entity that is "both S and P" would require its character to be simultaneously self-contained and externally constrained. This empties the dichotomy: S's self-determination and P's external-dependence cannot both govern the same character.

Note: An entity whose essence is eternal but whose existence is contingent (the Thomistic essence/existence distinction) does not constitute a third form. Character (R.9, Def.4) is the unified expression of all determinate properties, including mode of existence. If the entity's existence depends on external actualization, the entity IS P-form regardless of what its essence, considered in abstraction, might be.

*Dissolution:* Character is either self-determining or externally-determined. A third form would require character that is neither, which empties "character" (R.9) of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every substantive entity is either S (self-determining in character) or P (actualized through external condition).

**Def.5** — S (Substantive and Eternal) := substantive entity whose character is self-determined, not dependent on external actualization.

**Def.6** — P (Substantive and Particular) := substantive entity whose character is determined through actualization by external condition or principle.

---

**R.11** — Every particular entity necessarily references what it is not.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a particular entity that does not reference anything external to itself.

*Test:* A particular entity is actualized through external principle (R.10, Def.6). Its character is bounded — it is this-particular-thing, not other-things. To signify what it IS requires signifying what bounds it. A being cannot be signified as "this particular being" without simultaneous reference to what it is not, what it excludes, what it contrasts with.

This is constitutive of particularity itself. A particular thing's identity IS its boundary. To refer to a particular thing is to refer to its boundary. To refer to its boundary is to refer beyond itself.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires a particular entity whose identity does not involve boundary. But particularity IS boundedness (R.10). The candidate empties "particular" of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every particular entity necessarily references what bounds it, what it is not, what lies external to itself. Particularity is constitutively relational.

---

**R.12** — Every particular entity necessarily references beyond itself.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a particular entity E whose character is completely self-explaining without reference beyond itself.

*Test:* By R.11, every particular references what it is not. But does this exhaustively explain E's character? E's character IS determined — it did not determine itself (R.10, Def.6). Something brought E to be as it is. Either:

(a) An infinite causal regress: E's actualizer requires an actualizer, infinitely. But each member of the regress is P-form (R.10). No member of an infinite chain of P-form entities is self-determining. The chain contains no actualization source. An infinite chain of IOUs is not payment. The character of every member remains ungrounded.

(b) E actualizes itself: But self-actualizing entities are S-form (R.10), not P-form. E cannot be P and also be its own actualizer.

(c) E's actualizer is some principle external to the space of particulars — something that grounds all particulars without itself requiring explanation from beyond.

E's character cannot be self-contained within the space of particular beings.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires a particular entity whose character is self-explaining within the particular realm. Particulars do not explain themselves (R.10, Def.6). The candidate empties particularity of its defining feature.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every particular entity necessarily references beyond itself — beyond the space of particulars to some principle that grounds them.

---

**R.13** — A necessary ground of reality must be unrestricted generative capacity.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a fundamental ground that is restricted, limited, or determinate in its character.

*Test:* The ground must account for all substantive reality. Suppose the ground is restricted — it has determinate limits to what it can generate. Then what explains the restriction? Either:

(a) Something external imposed the limit: then that something is more fundamental. It becomes the true ground.

(b) The ground limited itself: then it contains the principle of its own limitation, which means it possesses unrestricted potential (the potential to limit itself). It was never truly restricted.

(c) The limit is a brute fact: but a ground whose character is unexplained is not a ground — it is another particular requiring explanation (R.12).

This is not an imported principle. It is derived. A restriction IS a determination (R.6). A determination of the ground makes the ground dependent on what determines it. A ground whose character is constituted by external imposition IS P-form (R.10, Def.6). A P-form ground IS not the ground (R.12). Every restriction makes the ground not-the-ground. No restriction survives.

Conversely: "Unrestricted" means possessing potential to generate any coherent being whatsoever, with no external constraint. Such a ground is S-form, self-determining, inexhaustible. Unrestricted character is coherent.

A distinction: unrestricted IS not indeterminate. The ground possesses character (R.9). Character IS determinate (R.6). The ground IS fully determinate in its nature — what it IS, IS unrestricted generative capacity. This determinacy IS self-determined (S-form, Def.5), not imposed from outside. What the derivation eliminates IS restriction — external constraint on what the ground can generate. What the derivation does not eliminate IS self-determined determinacy — the ground being fully what it IS. A ground that IS unrestricted AND fully determinate in its own nature IS not a contradiction. It IS what "self-determining" (Def.5) means applied to the ground: the ground's character IS its own, not imposed, and its character IS to generate without external limit.

What determines which possibilities are "coherent"? A possibility is coherent when its signification does not empty itself of content. This is the derivation's own method applied to the question of what the ground can generate. If an external principle determines coherence, the ground refers beyond itself. If the ground itself IS the standard of coherence, then "coherent" means "coherent with this ground's nature." Unrestricted capacity that generates only what is coherent with itself IS self-containing coherence.

*Dissolution:* Any restriction requires external explanation, which undermines the ground's status as fundamental.

*Surviving conclusion:* A necessary ground of reality must be unrestricted generative capacity — possessing potential to actualise any coherent possibility without external constraint.

**Def.7** — A (Anything) := the necessary ground of reality — unrestricted generative capacity possessing potential to actualise any coherent possibility without external constraint.

Remark on the name and what it names. Def.7 names the ground "Anything." The name is deliberate. It is the closest English word to what has been derived: that from which any coherent possibility can actualise. It does not say what the ground is like. It says: anything.

Every term the derivation applies to this ground ("unrestricted," "generative," "capacity," "necessary," "unique") is a description. These descriptions are accurate. They are what the eliminative method produces. But what they describe precedes them. The ground does not carry these terms the way a chair carries the property of being wooden. All determination comes after. The descriptions land because there is something for them to land on. What they land on was there before the landing.

The word "Anything" is the least presumptuous name available.

Rem. — Anaximander pointed here with his apeiron, the "boundless" (Kirk, Raven & Schofield, 1983). Aristotle reports his argument: "the Boundless has no origin, because it is itself the origin" (Physics 203b). Plotinus (Enneads V.4) and Schelling (1800) made later attempts at the same gesture. The derivation arrives through elimination at what they pointed toward through intuition. What classical theism calls "infinite perfection" and the Thomistic tradition calls "pure act" (Aquinas, Summa Theologica I, q.3–q.11) converges with R.13's result. The convergence is real. It is not the derivation's source.

**R.14** — A necessarily exists.

The ground cannot not obtain. The derivation calls this "necessary existence." The remark at R.13 applies: "exists" is the derivation's word. What it describes does not depend on the word.

*Candidate for dissolution:* A as contingent or non-existent.

*Test:* By R.12, all particular beings reference beyond themselves. By R.13, this reference terminates in unrestricted generative capacity. Either A exists or it does not.

If A does not exist: no ground exists. But R.12 establishes that particular beings require a ground. Ungrounded particular beings would be self-actualizing, making them S-form (R.10), not P-form. P-form without anything to depend on empties P-form of its content.

If A exists contingently: it requires actualization from an external ground A'. But A is unrestricted (R.13). If A' can actualize unrestricted capacity, A' must itself be unrestricted. A' is A. A cannot be actualized by something other than itself. It is self-actualizing, hence S-form (R.10), hence necessary.

*Dissolution:* A's non-existence empties R.12. A's contingency collapses A' into A.

*Surviving conclusion:* A necessarily exists.

Rem. — The cosmological argument, from Aristotle's unmoved mover through Aquinas's five ways, sought a necessary ground for contingent beings. The derivation arrives at a structural version of this.

---

**R.15** — A is unique.

*Candidate for dissolution:* reality containing multiple unrestricted generative capacities.

*Test:* Suppose A1 and A2 are both unrestricted. If they are distinct, they constrain each other — each limits the space of what the other can actualize. Neither is truly unrestricted. If they are identical in every respect, they are not multiple but one.

*Dissolution:* Unrestricted capacity cannot coexist with another unrestricted capacity. The candidate empties "unrestricted" of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* A is unique. Only one unrestricted generative capacity exists.

Rem. — Monotheism is derived. One ground. The derivation does not import this from any tradition. It derives uniqueness from what "unrestricted" means. Polytheism dissolves: multiple grounds, each described as unrestricted, restrict each other; multiple grounds, each restricted, are not the ground (R.13).

---

**R.16** — Every substantive form reducible to combinations of S and A reduces into A.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a substantive entity combining S-form and P-form properties that does not reduce to either.

*Test:* Every substantive entity is either S or P (R.10).

Case 1: An entity self-determining in some properties but dependent in others. Character is unified (R.9). If any part depends, the whole depends. The entity is P-form.

Case 2: An entity that is A itself. By R.15, A is unique.

Case 3: An entity that actualises through A. By R.12, this is P-form.

*Dissolution:* Entities cannot be simultaneously self-determining and dependent. The candidate empties either S or P of its meaning.

*Surviving conclusion:* Every substantive entity is either A (unique unrestricted capacity) or a particular being dependent on A for actualization. No intermediate or mixed forms obtain.

**R.17** — The relationships defined in R.10–R.16 hold necessarily across all possible realities.

In any possible reality where beings exist substantively, they either depend on actualization from beyond themselves or they do not. This is logically necessary.

The necessity of A follows from R.12 (particular beings require a ground) and R.13 (the ground must be unrestricted). The uniqueness of A follows from R.15 (unrestricted capacity excludes multiplicity).

These relationships depend on what substantive reality IS, not on the specific laws or features of any particular universe.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires a possible reality violating the constitutive structure of substantive being. The candidate requires what obtains to not have the structure it necessarily has.

*Surviving conclusion:* The relationships among S-form, P-form, and A hold necessarily in all possible realities where substantive being obtains.

Phase II is complete. The ground of reality is unrestricted generative capacity (R.13). It necessarily exists (R.14). It is unique (R.15). Everything is either the ground or dependent on the ground (R.16). These results hold across all possible realities (R.17). The derivation names the ground "Anything." ## Phase III — Actualisation Through Permission and Will

Anything necessarily exists (R.14). It is unrestricted generative capacity (R.13). Does it actualise? If so, how? This phase derives: Anything must actualise. Actualisation is neither forced nor random. It is Permissive. Permission operates through Will — the being's own self-determining act. Will is self-grounding, time-creating, and preservative of Anything's nature. Anything maintains unity across difference. All beings possess will.

**R.18** — A's generative capacity IS actualised.

*Candidate for dissolution:* A's capacity remaining forever unactualized.

*Test:* A is unrestricted generative capacity (R.13, Def.7). Capacity is the potential to generate. Unrestricted capacity is capacity without limit. If A's capacity remains forever unactualized, A possesses potential for generation but never generates. Not to actualize would be to fail to exercise what capacity means. This would limit A (restricting it to non-actualization), emptying "unrestricted" of its content.

A is the ground of reality (R.14). If A never actualizes, nothing actualizes. But particular beings exist (R.12). This ground is A (R.13). A necessarily actualizes.

*Dissolution:* Unrestricted generative capacity that never generates empties "generative capacity" of its meaning.

*Surviving conclusion:* A's generative capacity is actualised. A generates beings.

**R.19** — Either actualisation is necessary, random, or permissive.

*Candidate for dissolution:* actualisation occurring through some fourth mode.

*Test:* When A actualizes, something determines which possibilities become actual. The determining principle is either:

(a) Necessary — A must actualize exactly as it does. Some constraint makes actualization determined.

(b) Random — A actualizes without any determining principle.

(c) Permissive — A permits actualisation through a principle that does not force and is not absent.

Two successive binaries exhaust the space. First: either a determining principle governs actualisation, or none does. If none, that IS Randomness. Second: if a determining principle governs, either it forces the outcome, or it does not. Forcing IS Necessity. Not forcing IS Permission. A fourth mode would require a third value in one of these binaries. Each binary is exhaustive by signification.

*Dissolution:* Three modes exhaust the two binaries. The candidate's signification empties at the point where it must specify what the fourth mode IS.

*Surviving conclusion:* Actualisation proceeds through one of three modes: Necessary, Random, or Permissive.

**Def.8** — Necessary actualization := actualization that is forced by constraint, leaving no alternative.

**Def.9** — Random actualization := actualization without any determining principle, occurring by chance.

**Def.10** — Permissive actualization := actualization that is neither forced nor random — allowed through a principle that does not determine but permits.

---

**R.20** — Actualisation is not Necessary.

*Candidate for dissolution:* actualisation as Necessary (forced by constraint).

*Test:* A is unrestricted generative capacity (R.13). If actualization is Necessary, A's actualizations are constrained by what forces them. Constraint means limitation. A cannot be both unrestricted and constrained by necessity.

If the determining principle is external to A, A is limited by it, emptying "unrestricted" of its content. If it is internal (A determined by its own nature to actualize exactly as it does), unrestricted capacity is restricted to one necessary actualization, emptying what "unrestricted capacity" signifies.

*Dissolution:* Unrestricted capacity determined by necessity empties "unrestricted" of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Actualisation is not Necessary.

---

**R.21** — Actualisation is not Random.

*Candidate for dissolution:* actualisation as Random (without any determining principle).

*Test:* A actualizes beings (R.18). Each being actualizes as determinate (R.5, R.6). For a being to be determinate, something must account for its determinacy. If randomness governs actualization, nothing accounts for the determinacy. But actual beings are determinate. Something must account for this. Random actualization leaves it unaccounted.

A is the ground of reality (R.14). A's actualizations are determinations of what IS. A's actualization cannot be groundless while A itself is the ground.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires actualizations without principle while also being determinate. Determinacy requires a determining principle. The candidate empties "determinate" of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Actualisation is not Random.

**R.22** — Actualisation is Permissive.

*Candidate for dissolution:* actualisation through some other mode than Permission.

*Test:* By R.19, actualisation is Necessary, Random, or Permissive. By R.20, not Necessary. By R.21, not Random. Permission is the remaining surviving mode.

Permission is not merely what survives by dissolution. It signifies positively. What does actualisation look like when neither forced nor random? The actualising principle A does not determine which specific beings actualise (preserving A's unrestrictedness), yet what actualises is determinate (not random). This is possible only if the actualising being itself supplies the determining principle. A furnishes the generative capacity. The being furnishes the determining principle. This IS Permission.

*Dissolution:* Necessity and Randomness dissolve (R.20, R.21). Permission is the only mode whose signification does not empty itself of content.

*Surviving conclusion:* Actualisation is Permissive. A furnishes capacity; the actualising being supplies its own determining principle.

**Def.11** — Permission := the mode of actualisation in which Anything furnishes unrestricted generative capacity and the actualising being furnishes the determining principle — actualisation that is determinate without external constraint and grounded without randomness. Permission IS what Anything IS: unrestricted generative capacity that furnishes without constraining.

Rem. — "The actualising being" IS not restricted to the being that comes into being. Anything furnishes unrestricted generative capacity (R.22). The determining principle IS furnished by the being whose willing actualises. This being CAN be the being that comes into being (willing itself into being through Anything). It CAN also be a being whose generative action brings into being what wills to be. In both cases, Anything furnishes capacity and a being furnishes the determining principle. Anything IS Permissive in both cases.

Rem. — Permission IS the mode of actualisation, not a causal mechanism. A causal mechanism would be a process by which one event produces another. Permission IS not a process. It IS the structural relationship between Anything (furnishes capacity) and the being (furnishes the determining principle). No causal chain connects them. The being's willing IS not caused by Anything. The being's willing IS the being's own self-determining act (R.23, Def.12), operative within what Anything furnishes. The question "what is the mechanism?" presupposes that actualisation IS a causal process. The derivation establishes that actualisation IS Permissive. Permissive actualisation IS not caused actualisation.

Rem. — Whitehead's process philosophy derives divine action as persuasive rather than coercive (Whitehead, 1929). The derivation arrives at Permission through elimination (R.20–R.22), not through Whitehead. The convergence IS noted: what Whitehead calls persuasion and what the derivation calls Permission share the structural feature of non-coercive furnishing. The route is different. The result converges.

**R.23** — Permission operates through Will.

*Candidate for dissolution:* Permission operating without a specific principle of differentiation.

*Test:* Permission requires a determining principle furnished by the being itself (R.22). Three candidates:

(a) External determination — something outside the being determines what it becomes. This reintroduces Necessity (R.20).

(b) No determination — nothing determines what the being becomes. This IS Randomness (R.21).

(c) Self-determination — the being's own nature determines what it becomes. The being differentiates itself from potential through its own act. The being wills and IS.

Only (c) survives. What is self-determination as an ontological principle? It is the being's own act by which it constitutes what it IS. This IS what Will means at the ontological level: the self-determining act by which differentiated being arises from undifferentiated potential. Will does not select from alternatives. Will constitutes. The being wills and what it wills IS.

Note: Will is not psychological deliberation. Psychological deliberation (weighing options, considering alternatives) is an expression that appears within the interference pattern as will's inversed form — choice (R.65 onward). Will at the ontological level is the self-determining act inherent in any actualisation whatever, whether conscious or not. A crystal's lattice structure is not imposed from outside (that would be Necessity, dissolved at R.20) nor random (dissolved at R.21); it is the mineral's own nature willing itself into being through Permission.

A further distinction: the Will derived here IS not Schopenhauer's Will. Schopenhauer (1818) posited Will as blind, irrational, ceaseless striving. The derivation's Will IS self-determining (Def.12), self-grounding (R.24), and operative within Permission (R.22). The shared word names structurally different concepts. Schopenhauer's Will IS closer to what the derivation derives at R.68 as will inverted into choice: striving without ground, generating without arrival.

*Dissolution:* (a) reintroduces Necessity. (b) reintroduces Randomness. Both are dissolved. Only (c) signifies.

*Surviving conclusion:* Permission operates through self-determination. Self-determination IS Will.

**Def.12** — Will := the being's own self-determining act — the power by which potential constitutes itself as actual. Not selection from alternatives (that is choice, will's inversed form — R.65 onward) but the self-constituting act of being.

**R.24** — Will is self-grounding.

*Candidate for dissolution:* Will as requiring a reason external to itself.

*Test:* If Will requires an external reason R, then R constrains Will. An external constraint on the transition principle reintroduces Necessity (R.20). If Will operates without any reason, that IS Randomness (R.21). Will is neither externally grounded nor groundless. The only remaining status: Will grounds itself. The willing IS its own reason for being this willing.

Note: Permission (R.22) describes the mode of actualisation; Will (R.23) is the operative principle within that mode. Will does not derive from Permission as from an external ground — Permission IS what actualisation looks like when the being supplies its own determining principle, and Will IS that self-supplying. They are the same structure under two descriptions. Self-grounding does not generate circularity; it is the recognition that the operative principle of non-forced, non-random actualisation IS self-determination, and self-determination is complete in itself.

*Dissolution:* External grounding reintroduces Necessity. No grounding reintroduces Randomness.

*Surviving conclusion:* Will is self-grounding — its own complete reason, requiring no justification from beyond itself.

**Def.13** — Self-grounding := the property of an act whose occurrence IS its own complete reason — not externally justified and not groundless, but self-complete.

**R.25** — Will does not occur within time; Will establishes time.

*Candidate for dissolution:* the first act of will as occurring within a pre-existing temporal sequence.

*Test:* Time signifies as the structure of succession — before and after, ordering of states. Before the first differentiation, A is undifferentiated potential (Def.7). Undifferentiated potential has no distinct states to order. Where there is no succession, there is no time. The first act of will IS the first differentiation (R.23, Def.12). The first act of will creates the first distinction between what was undifferentiated and what is now differentiated — the first "before" and "after." This first succession IS the beginning of time.

If the first act of will occurred within a pre-existing time, there would already be distinct states. But distinct states require prior differentiation, which requires prior willing. This generates an infinite regress with no terminus. But A IS undifferentiated potential (Def.7). The regress denies A's constitutive character.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires time before differentiation. Time's signification requires differentiation. The candidate requires what it purports to precede.

*Surviving conclusion:* Will does not occur within time. The first act of will establishes time by creating the first succession.

Rem.4 — "Before" and "after" in this resolution refer to logical, not temporal, ordering: undifferentiated potential has logical priority over differentiated actuality, but not temporal priority, because temporality IS the differentiation.

**R.26** — Anything necessarily maintains unity across difference.

*Candidate for dissolution:* Anything enabling differentiation without maintaining any connection among what it differentiates.

*Test:* All beings actualize through the same Anything from the same A (R.22, R.14, R.15). If beings are entirely disconnected from A, they exist independently of their source. But their existence IS through Anything from A (R.22–R.23). A principle that is operative in the being's coming-to-be yet leaves no trace is not operative. The act of signifying this candidate empties "actualises through" of its content.

If beings are entirely disconnected from one another, each actualizes through the same Anything yet shares nothing with the others. But sharing the same principle from the same unique source IS a structural commonality. To deny commonality while affirming shared origin empties "shared origin" of its content.

Anything has two inseparable aspects: (1) it enables differentiation (R.22–R.23), and (2) it maintains connection among what it differentiates. These are not two principles but one.

*Dissolution:* Shared origin without structural relation empties "shared origin" of what it constitutively entails.

*Surviving conclusion:* Anything necessarily maintains unity across difference. Enabling distinct existence while maintaining connection to the source and among beings. Anything's two aspects — differentiation and unity — are constitutively inseparable.

Rem. — Anything's nature includes two constitutively inseparable aspects: (1) enabling differentiation — furnishing unrestricted generative capacity without constraining what actualises; (2) maintaining connection — sustaining structural relation among what it differentiates. One nature, dual character.

**R.27** — What actualises through a principle participates in that principle.

*Candidate for dissolution:* an entity E that actualises through principle P but does not participate in P.

*Test:* "Actualises through P" signifies that P is operative in E's coming-to-be. P is the principle by which E exists at all. For P to be operative in E's coming-to-be yet absent from E's nature, E would have to come-to-be through P without P shaping what E IS. But coming-to-be IS the acquiring of a nature. If P governs how E comes to be, P governs what E comes to be. To deny P's presence in E's nature while affirming P's role empties "coming-to-be through P" of its content.

*Dissolution:* Existence and nature are not separable for actualised beings. A principle that leaves no trace is not a principle through which the entity actualised.

*Surviving conclusion:* What actualises through a principle participates in that principle.

**R.28** — All beings necessarily possess will.

*Candidate for dissolution:* a being that actualises through Will yet lacks will.

*Test:* All beings actualise through Will (R.22–R.23). By R.27, what actualises through Will participates in Will. Will IS the being's own self-determining act (Def.12). To participate in Will IS to possess the self-determining capacity through which the being IS. A being that participates in Will but lacks will participates in a capacity without possessing it — a signification that empties "participates in" of its content.

This is not a contingent feature that A could have withheld. Will IS the transition principle (R.22). All actualisation occurs through it. All actualised beings bear it in their nature (R.27).

Note: Will is ontological, not phenomenological. A non-conscious being (a mineral, a field, a particle) possesses will in the ontological sense: its actualisation IS self-determination from potential. Will at this level signifies the inherent self-determining act by which ANY being IS this being rather than some other.

A distinction: will's self-groundedness (R.24) refers to the being's determining principle. The being determines WHAT it is through its own self-grounding willing. The being's EXISTENCE remains dependent on Anything's furnishing (R.10, R.22, Def.11). Dependence in existence and self-determination in willing are not contradictory. They are what Permission (Def.11) structurally produces.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires actualisation through Will without participation in Will. R.27 establishes that actualisation through P entails participation in P. The candidate empties R.27 of its content.

*Surviving conclusion:* All beings necessarily possess will.

**R.29** — Will preserves A's unrestricted nature.

*Candidate for dissolution:* Will diminishing or restricting A by actualising potentials.

*Test:* If Will, in actualising potentials, eliminates others, then A is no longer unrestricted after the first actualisation. But A's unrestrictedness is necessary (R.14). If Will restricted A, Will would empty its own foundation of content. A transition principle that undermines its own foundation does not signify as a principle.

Unrestricted generative capacity is not a finite store depleted by use. Unactualised potentials remain as potentials. No possibility space is closed. A capacity exhausted by use was never unrestricted.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires A's nature to change through Will's operation. A's nature IS unrestricted generative capacity (Def.7), and this is necessary (R.14). The candidate dissolves against R.14.

*Surviving conclusion:* Will preserves A's unrestricted nature. Actualisation does not diminish the source.

Phase III is complete. Anything actualises (R.18). Actualisation is Permissive (R.22): Anything furnishes; beings determine. Permission operates through Will (R.23). Will is self-grounding (R.24), time-creating (R.25), preservative of Anything's unrestricted nature (R.29). Anything maintains unity across difference (R.26). All beings possess will (R.28). ## Phase IV — Coherence Through Anything's Nature

Distinct beings actualise through Will. Each possesses will. Each is bounded, particular, different from the others. What holds them together? This phase derives: the coherence principle must be non-coercive, principle-based, and immanent. Only non-coercive Union operating through Anything's nature satisfies all requirements. The same principle that actualises also maintains coherence.

**R.30** — Differentiated reality requires a principle maintaining unity among distinct beings.

*Candidate for dissolution:* differentiated reality coherent without connection among its beings.

*Test:* Beings actualise through Will (R.22–R.23), producing distinct entities. Each is particular — bounded, distinct from others (R.11). If beings are entirely disconnected, each operates in isolation. Isolation means no interaction, no influence, no relation. If beings cannot affect one another, they cannot cohere into a unified reality. The same A grounds all beings (R.14, R.15), and all partake of the same Will principle (R.27). This shared origin IS coherence. But if beings share origin with no ongoing principle maintaining connection, they fragment after actualization. Actual reality shows coherence that persists. This requires an active principle.

*Dissolution:* Differentiation without connection produces fragmentation. The candidate empties "reality" of its signification as unified being.

*Surviving conclusion:* Differentiated reality requires an active principle maintaining unity among its distinct beings.

---

**R.31** — The coherence principle must be non-coercive.

*Candidate for dissolution:* coherence maintained through coercive union or fusion.

*Test:* Beings possess will (R.28). Coercion overrides will. A principle that eliminates will eliminates the being's fundamental character. Coercive union erases the distinctness of beings — merging them into undifferentiated unity. But differentiation IS constitutive of actualized being (R.23). Erasing distinctness erases actualization.

*Dissolution:* Coercive unity violates will and erases actualisation.

*Surviving conclusion:* The coherence principle must be non-coercive. It must maintain connection while preserving will and distinctness.

---

**R.32** — The coherence principle is not randomness.

*Candidate for dissolution:* coherence as arising by chance.

*Test:* Coherence IS order — persistent patterns, stable relationships, regular structure. Randomness produces neither pattern nor persistence. What arises by chance dissolves by chance. Actual reality exhibits coherence that persists and builds. Random connection cannot account for this.

*Dissolution:* Persistent coherence without principle empties "coherence" of its signification.

*Surviving conclusion:* The coherence principle is not randomness.

---

**R.33** — The coherence principle must be non-external.

*Candidate for dissolution:* coherence imposed from outside the totality of actual beings.

*Test:* By R.15, A is unique and unrestricted. There is nothing outside A or the realm of beings actualised by A. External imposition requires what does not exist. More fundamentally: coherence externally imposed violates the autonomy of beings actualised through Will.

*Dissolution:* Nothing external exists, and external imposition violates will.

*Surviving conclusion:* The coherence principle must be immanent — arising from within the totality of A and actualised beings.

---

**R.34** — The coherence principle is non-coercive Union.

*Candidate for dissolution:* coherence maintained through any principle other than non-coercive Union.

*Test:* By R.31, not coercive. By R.32, not random. By R.33, not external. What remains? A principle that maintains connection without forcing, is based on determining principle (not chance), and arises from within the totality. This IS Union — connection maintained through recognition of shared source, allowing each being its distinctness while relating it to others through common origin.

*Dissolution:* Coercion, randomness, and external imposition are the only alternatives. All fail.

*Surviving conclusion:* The coherence principle IS non-coercive Union.

**Def.15** — Union := the principle maintaining coherence among distinct beings through recognition of shared source — non-coercive, enabling distinctness while sustaining connection.

**R.35** — Union is not identity or fusion.

*Candidate for dissolution:* Union as requiring beings to lose distinctness or become identical.

*Test:* Beings are actualised as distinct (R.11, R.23). Union maintains these beings in relation (R.34). For Union to require identity or fusion would require distinct beings to cease being distinct. But this undoes actualization. Two beings that are identical are not in relation — there is only one being. Relation requires two. Union relates distinct beings. If Union erased distinctness, it would erase what it relates.

*Dissolution:* The candidate requires Union to eliminate distinctness. Union maintains the distinctness it unites.

*Surviving conclusion:* Union is not identity or fusion. It is connection among distinct beings who maintain their distinctness.

**R.36** — Union necessarily operates through Anything's nature.

*Candidate for dissolution:* Union operating through principle other than Anything.

*Test:* Union relates distinct beings (R.34, R.35). What grounds this relating? Three candidates:

(a) Self-interest — each being maintains connection for its own benefit. Self-interest in isolation produces conflict and fragmentation.

(b) Imposed obligation — beings forced to maintain connection. This violates will (R.31) and is external (R.33).

By R.26, Anything necessarily maintains unity across difference. Union through Anything is not a second principle added to actualisation; it is Anything's dual character (R.26) operative among distinct beings.

*Dissolution:* Self-interest produces fragmentation. Imposed obligation violates will. Anything's nature is the only principle unifying distinct beings while preserving their nature.

*Surviving conclusion:* Union necessarily operates through Anything's nature — oriented toward the other's flourishing for its own sake.

**Def.16** — Anything (as operative among beings) := oriented toward the other's flourishing as other, not for self-benefit but for the other's sake, enabling distinct beings to cohere while maintaining their distinctness. This IS Anything's nature (R.26) operative among distinct beings.

Maintaining unity with a being whose existence IS through Anything's furnishing IS sustaining the conditions under which that being generates. Phase IV is complete. Coherence operates through non-coercive Union (R.34), which IS Anything's nature operative among beings (R.36, Def.16).
